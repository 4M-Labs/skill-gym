# Cold Email - LocalizeAI to Amanda Foster

**Skill used:** cold-email
**Framework:** Trigger > Problem > Proof > Ask

---

## Context

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Prospect** | Amanda Foster, Head of Product |
| **Company** | BrightPath Education (100-person edtech) |
| **Trigger** | Expanding into 3 new countries simultaneously |
| **Product** | LocalizeAI — AI-powered content localization platform |
| **Proof** | LearnGlobal (80-person edtech) went from 2 to 5 countries in 6 months with 40% faster time-to-market |
| **Goal** | Get a reply |

---

## Subject Line

`new market content`

*Why it works: 3 words, lowercase, looks internal — like something a colleague flagged about an ops challenge. No pitch, no urgency, no Amanda's first name. Gets opened.*

---

## Email

Amanda,

Expanding into 3 countries at once means every product flow, help doc, and onboarding sequence needs to work in 3 new languages — probably on a deadline your localization workflow wasn't built for.

We helped LearnGlobal through a similar push. 80-person edtech, went from 2 countries to 5 in 6 months. Before LocalizeAI, their product team was bottlenecked on manual translation — engineers waiting on translators, launch dates slipping. After, they shipped localized product content 40% faster than their old process.

The part that mattered wasn't just speed. It was that product could actually hit the expansion timeline without turning localization into a permanent fire drill.

If keeping BrightPath's expansion on schedule without your team drowning in translation coordination is a priority right now, happy to share what worked. No pitch — just what an 80-person edtech did a few sizes smaller than you.

Worth a quick look when it makes sense on your end?

---

## Quality Check

- **Human-sounding?** Yes. Read aloud: conversational, uses contractions, no marketing-speak. No "I hope this email finds you well" or "I came across your profile." Reads like a peer who understands product operations.
- **Would I reply?** Yes. The problem is specific and immediate — expanding into 3 countries means product content localization is actively on her plate right now. The proof is concrete and from a comparable edtech company.
- **Every sentence serves the reader?** Yes. Line 1 reflects her current situation (product content across 3 new markets, deadline pressure). Lines 2-4 tell a story about a company like hers. Line 5 names the real outcome (no more fire drills). Line 6 is the ask.
- **Personalization connected to problem?** Yes. "3 countries at once" ties directly to the product localization bottleneck — if you removed that opening, the email loses its reason for existing. "100-person edtech" is implied through the LearnGlobal parallel.
- **One clear ask?** Yes. "Worth a quick look when it makes sense on your end?" — interest-based, no meeting request, no calendar link. Easy to reply yes with a one-liner.
- **No forbidden patterns?** No "I hope this email finds you well," no "My name is X and I work at Y," no jargon, no feature dump, no HTML/images, no fake urgency, no "leverage" or "synergy."

---

## Why This Works for Head of Product

- **Product-content framing, not marketing:** A CMO cares about campaigns and brand consistency. A Head of Product cares about product flows, help docs, onboarding sequences — the content that's embedded in the product itself. The email speaks to her world, not a marketer's.
- **"Engineers waiting on translators":** This is the operational pain a Head of Product actually feels. It's not abstract "localization challenges" — it's the specific blocker that delays launches and frustrates the engineering team.
- **"Without your team drowning in translation coordination":** Product heads manage cross-functional coordination. This line names the coordination tax directly — it's the thing that makes localization feel like a fire drill instead of a process.
- **"Product could actually hit the expansion timeline":** This is the outcome a Head of Product owns. She's measured on shipping on schedule. The email connects localization to that metric, not to marketing efficiency.
- **Parallel is tight:** LearnGlobal (80-person edtech) is close enough to BrightPath (100-person edtech) to feel directly relevant. Going from 2 to 5 countries mirrors the 3-country expansion. The 40% metric is specific without being hard to believe.

## What I'd A/B Test

- **Subject line variant:** `3 countries, 3 languages`
- **Opening variant:** Lead with the proof first: "LearnGlobal went from 2 to 5 countries in 6 months — product content in 40% less time. Curious if the same approach works at BrightPath's scale."
- **CTA variant:** "Open to 15 minutes to compare notes on how product teams handle multi-market launches at your size?"
- **Angle variant:** Lead with engineering frustration instead of timeline — if you have a signal that BrightPath's engineering team is vocal about translation delays (tech blog posts, hiring for localization engineers), that's a sharper emotional trigger for a Head of Product than general schedule pressure.
