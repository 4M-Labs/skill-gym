Written using the Cold Email Skill v2.0.0 framework.

## Context

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|

## Email

### Subject Line

`backend hiring`

### Email Body

Marcus —

You're hiring 3 backend engineers, which usually means your current team is underwater on infra.

When MedFlow was at 40 people with a similar team size, their 2-person backend was spending almost a full day each week just keeping deploys running. Deployments took 2 hours and failed often enough that nobody wanted to push on Fridays.

They moved to CloudDeploy. Deployments went from 2 hours to 12 minutes. That freed up roughly an engineer-day per week — which they used to actually ship product instead of babysitting pipelines.

Worth a quick look?

## Annotations

| Element | Choice | Principle Applied |
|---------|--------|-------------------|
| Subject line: "backend hiring" | 2 words, lowercase, internal-looking. References a real signal (job posting) without being creepy about it. | Subject Lines: short, boring, internal-looking |
| Opening: "You're hiring 3 backend engineers" | Leads with their world, not ours. Shows research without showing off. The observation connects directly to the problem. | Lead with their world; Personalization must connect to the problem |
| "which usually means your current team is underwater on infra" | Bridges from the observation to the problem. Uses "usually" to normalize — not accusatory, just pattern recognition. | Observation → Problem |
| MedFlow case study with specific numbers | "40 people," "2-person backend," "almost a full day each week," "2 hours to 12 minutes" — specifics beat claims. | Proof with concrete data; One proof point beats ten features |
| "nobody wanted to push on Fridays" | Adds a human detail that makes the story real. Technical readers will nod. | Write like a peer, not a vendor |
| "which they used to actually ship product instead of babysitting pipelines" | Reframes the value from "faster deploys" to "more product output." CTOs care about throughput, not tooling. | Benefits over features |
| "Worth a quick look?" | Low-friction, interest-based CTA. No meeting request. Easy to say yes with a one-line reply. | One ask, low friction |
| No "I hope this email finds you well" | Avoids the #1 cold email red flag. Gets straight to the point. | What to Avoid |
| No signature block with title/company | Keeps it minimal. The email should feel like a note from a peer, not a sales pitch. | Voice: smart colleague, not vendor |
| "Marcus —" (em dash, not comma) | Casual, peer-level greeting. No "Dear" or "Hi first name." | C-suite: ultra-brief, peer-level, understated |

## Alternatives

### Subject Line Options

**Option A:** `backend hiring` — *Recommended. References the research signal directly. Looks internal, not promotional.*

**Option B:** `infra at 50 people` — *Broader. Works if you don't have a hiring signal but know their company size.*

**Option C:** `deploy pipeline` — *Technical. Better if the CTO is hands-on and owns infrastructure decisions.*

### Opening Line Options

**Option A:** You're hiring 3 backend engineers, which usually means your current team is underwater on infra. — *Recommended. Connects the signal to the problem in one sentence.*

**Option B:** At 50 people with a backend team that's probably still single-digit, infrastructure tends to become the bottleneck. — *More analytical. Works for a CTO who thinks in systems.*

**Option C:** Saw you're scaling the backend team — congrats on the Series A. That growth usually comes with infra growing pains. — *Adds a congrats. Warmer, but slightly longer.*

### CTA Options

**Option A:** Worth a quick look? — *Recommended. Lowest friction. Easy to reply "sure" or "tell me more."*

**Option B:** Want me to send over what MedFlow's setup looks like? — *Offers value instead of asking for commitment. Good if the proof point landed.*

**Option C:** Open to a 5-minute comparison? — *Slightly more committal. Use only if the email length and proof are strong enough to justify it.*
