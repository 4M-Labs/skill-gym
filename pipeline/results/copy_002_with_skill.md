## Annotations

### Hero Section

**Headline — "Stop Stressing About What's For Dinner"**  
Mirrors the Facebook ad hook exactly. Campaign message match is critical for landing page conversion — the visitor clicked expecting this message, so we deliver it front and center. Applies the principle: *Match headline to ad/traffic source*.

**Subheadline — "MealPrep AI creates a personalized weekly meal plan in 30 seconds..."**  
Expands the headline with specificity. Three concrete details (personalized, 30 seconds, based on real life) replace vague claims. Applies: *Specificity over vagueness* and *Clarity over cleverness*.

**CTA — "Start My Free Trial"**  
Action-oriented, communicates what they get, includes risk reversal qualifier. Stronger than "Sign Up" or "Get Started." Follows the CTA formula: [Action Verb] + [What They Get].

---

### Problem Section

**"You Know the Feeling"**  
Rhetorical empathy opener. The reader sees themselves in the scenario before we pitch the solution. Applies: *Use rhetorical questions* and *Customer language over company language*.

**The 5:30 PM scenario**  
Uses vivid, specific imagery rather than abstract pain points. "Partner texting 'what's for dinner?'" is more relatable than "decision fatigue." Applies: *Show over tell*.

**"You don't need another recipe blog"**  
Names the real frustration — being overwhelmed by content — and positions the product as the antidote. Applies: *Address objections directly*.

---

### Benefits Section

**"Your Personal Meal Planner, Powered by AI"**  
Reframes "AI" from a tech buzzword into a benefit. The reader doesn't care about AI; they care about having a personal planner. Applies: *Benefits over features*.

**Each benefit follows the pattern: What it does → What that means for you.**  
Example: "Uses what you already have" (feature) → "Less waste. Fewer impulse grocery runs" (benefit). Applies: *Connect feature → benefit → outcome*.

---

### How It Works

**Three-step structure**  
Reduces perceived complexity. Three steps feel achievable. Four or more starts to feel like work. Applies: *Reduce perceived complexity*.

**"Done before the kettle boils"**  
Analogy makes the 30-second claim tangible and memorable. Applies: *Use analogies when helpful*.

## Alternatives

### Headline Options

**Option A: Stop Stressing About What's For Dinner**  
— Direct match to the Facebook ad hook. Maximizes message match and reduces bounce from "am I in the right place?" anxiety. *Recommended.*

**Option B: Your Weekly Meal Plan, Done in 30 Seconds**  
— Leads with the speed/outcome benefit instead of the pain point. Stronger for audiences who already know they want meal planning. May lose the emotional hook.

**Option C: What If Dinner Was Already Figured Out?**  
— Curiosity-driven opener. Makes the reader pause and imagine. Stronger for cold traffic, but less direct than Option A for paid campaigns.

---

### CTA Options

**Option A: Start My Free Trial**  
— Clear, action-oriented, includes risk reversal. Best for paid traffic where trust is low. *Recommended.*

**Option B: Get My First Meal Plan**  
— Specific outcome-focused. Emphasizes what they receive, not what they do. Good alternative if the trial framing feels too committed.

**Option C: Plan This Week's Dinners Free**  
— Ultra-specific and immediate. Creates urgency ("this week") and removes cost objection ("free"). Could outperform for impulse-driven clicks.

---

### Subheadline Options

**Option A: MealPrep AI creates a personalized weekly meal plan in 30 seconds — based on what you'll actually eat, what you can afford, and what's already in your fridge.**  
— Three concrete details in one sentence. Matches the specificity principle. *Recommended.*

**Option B: Tell us what your family eats. We'll plan the rest.**  
— Shorter, warmer, lower cognitive load. Trades specificity for simplicity. Better for audiences who scan rather than read.
