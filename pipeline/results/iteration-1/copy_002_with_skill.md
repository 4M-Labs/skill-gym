## Annotations

### Why these headline choices:

**"Stop stressing about what's for dinner"** — Directly mirrors the Facebook ad hook. This is the rule of message match: when someone clicks an ad, the landing page should feel like a continuation, not a new conversation. Using the exact pain point from the ad reduces cognitive friction and bounces.

**"Tell us what's in your fridge. We'll tell you what to make."** — The subheadline immediately answers "how?" after the headline answers "why?" It's specific and tangible, not vague promises about "revolutionizing meal planning."

### Key copy decisions:

**Pain Section placement:** I put the pain section before the solution because the target audience (busy parents) is emotionally reactive to being seen in their struggle. The phrase "you know the drill" creates instant recognition. The list format mirrors the chaotic mental state of someone who hasn't figured out dinner yet.

**"30 minutes" specificity:** Instead of "quick" or "fast," I anchored to a specific time. Parents know exactly how long 30 minutes feels versus 15 versus 90. This builds trust because it's verifiable.

**Testimonials with details:** Each testimonial includes specific numbers ($340 saved), specific details (gluten-free husband, picky daughter), and emotional truth (cried in the grocery store). Generic "love this app!" testimonials don't convert.

**"Less than a takeout dinner"** price framing: $7.99 is already low, but anchoring it to a takeout dinner (which this audience buys often) makes it feel like a no-brainer trade-off.

**14-day free trial guarantee:** Removes risk at the exact moment price objection appears. The "no guilt" language mirrors the brand voice and speaks to parent guilt directly.

**Social proof bar at top:** Showing 12,000+ parents before the CTA creates legitimacy for a paid traffic source. Cold traffic needs proof they're not the first to try this.

## Alternatives

### Headline Option 1: "What's for dinner? We already know."
**Rationale:** Positions the app as omniscient and confident. Short, punchy, answers the universal parent question. Could work for higher-trust audiences who already know the brand.

### Headline Option 2: "The 10-second answer to 'what's for dinner?'"
**Rationale:** Emphasizes speed of the solution. Works if the ad targets the decision-fatigue angle. Risk: "10-second" might feel hyperbolic if the app actually takes longer.

### Headline Option 3: "Your fridge is full. Your calendar is empty. Let's fix dinner."
**Rationale:** Scene-setting headline that speaks to the specific moment of staring into the fridge. More literary, might work better for Instagram or Pinterest traffic than Facebook.

### Why I chose "Stop stressing about what's for dinner" as the primary:

1. **Message match** — mirrors the Facebook ad hook exactly
2. **Emotional resonance** — "stress" is the exact word this audience uses
3. **Specific** — names the exact moment (dinner), not a vague benefit
4. **Active voice** — "stop" is a command, not a passive suggestion
5. **Low cognitive load** — no metaphor to decode, just plain language
