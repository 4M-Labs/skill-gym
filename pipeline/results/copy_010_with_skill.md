## Annotations

### Headline

**"Travel without the guilt."** — Opens with the emotional core of the problem. The audience doesn't just worry about logistics; they carry guilt. Naming it directly builds immediate rapport.

**"Your pet deserves a sitter, not a cage."** — Draws the contrast between PetPal and kennels without disparaging kennels outright. Uses "deserves" to validate the pet owner's instincts — they already know their pet deserves better. The word "cage" is deliberate: it surfaces the mental image kennel users are trying to escape.

**Principle applied:** Customer language over company language. The audience thinks in terms of guilt and "my pet deserves better," not "premium pet care marketplace."

### Subheadline

**"Background-checked, pet first aid certified sitters"** — Stacks two concrete proof points back-to-back. Specificity builds trust. These aren't vague promises; they're verifiable facts.

**"treat your pet like family"** — Bridges the logical (vetting) to the emotional (care). This is the transformation: from transaction to relationship.

**"free video meet-and-greet before you book"** — Removes the biggest objection: "How do I know this stranger is safe?" The word "free" eliminates risk. "Before you book" signals control — the owner decides, not the platform.

**Principle applied:** Benefits over features. Background checks are a feature. "Knowing your pet is in hands you trust" is the benefit.

### Primary CTA

**"Find Your Pet's Perfect Sitter"** — Action-oriented ("Find"), specific ("Your Pet's Perfect Sitter"), and personal ("Your"). Avoids generic language like "Get Started" or "Sign Up." The word "perfect" implies curation, not a random list.

**Principle applied:** CTA formula — Action Verb + What They Get. The owner isn't signing up for a platform; they're finding a person their pet will love.
